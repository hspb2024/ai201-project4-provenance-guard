# Provenance Guard — Planning & Spec

Provenance Guard is a content-attribution service. A creator submits a piece of
text; the system estimates whether it was written by a human or generated by AI,
attaches a plain-English transparency label with a calibrated confidence score,
records the decision in a tamper-evident audit log, and lets the creator appeal a
result they believe is wrong.

---

## 1. Architecture Narrative (Milestone 1)

**The path a single piece of text takes, from submission to label:**

1. A creator sends `POST /submit` with `{ text, creator_id }`.
2. The **request layer** (Flask + Flask-Limiter) validates the body (text present,
   non-empty, under a size cap) and rate-limits the caller so the LLM signal can't
   be abused.
3. The text is passed to **Signal 1 — LLM classifier (Groq)**. Groq returns a JSON
   assessment: an AI-likelihood probability in `[0,1]` plus a one-line rationale.
   This captures *semantic / stylistic coherence* holistically.
4. The same text is passed to **Signal 2 — stylometric heuristics** (pure Python).
   It measures *structural* properties (sentence-length variance, vocabulary
   diversity, punctuation variety) and returns its own AI-likelihood in `[0,1]`.
5. The **scoring layer** combines the two signals into one calibrated
   `ai_likelihood` score and derives a `certainty` value and an `attribution`
   bucket (`likely_ai` / `uncertain` / `likely_human`).
6. The **label layer** maps the bucket + score to one of three plain-English
   transparency labels.
7. The **audit log** (SQLite) writes a structured, timestamped row: content id,
   creator id, both raw signal scores, combined score, attribution, label, status.
8. The response returns `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, `label`, and the
   per-signal breakdown. The creator keeps the `content_id`.
9. If the creator disagrees, they send `POST /appeal` with the `content_id` and a
   reason. The system flips that record's `status` to `appealed`, writes a new audit
   entry, and the record surfaces in the reviewer queue (`GET /appeals`).

**Components:** request layer (Flask), Signal 1 (Groq LLM), Signal 2 (stylometry),
scoring layer, label layer, audit log (SQLite), appeal handler, reviewer queue.

---

## 2. Detection Signals (Spec Question 1)

We use **two genuinely independent signals** — one semantic, one structural.

### Signal 1 — LLM classification (Groq)
- **Measures:** whether the text *reads* as human or AI — tone, coherence,
  hedging, the "too-even" register typical of instruction-tuned models.
- **Why it differs:** AI text is optimized for fluent, low-risk, average prose;
  human writing carries idiosyncrasy, voice, and local inconsistency the model
  can perceive holistically.
- **Output:** JSON `{ "ai_likelihood": 0.0–1.0, "rationale": "<one line>" }`.
- **Blind spot:** heavily edited AI text, or AI prompted to imitate a specific
  voice, can fool it. It is also non-deterministic and can be over-confident.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)
- **Measures three structural properties**, each normalized to an AI-likelihood
  contribution in `[0,1]`, then averaged:
  - **Burstiness / sentence-length variance** — humans vary sentence length a lot;
    AI is more uniform. Low variance ⇒ higher AI-likelihood.
  - **Type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity)** — measured on a fixed window so
    length doesn't distort it. Very smooth, mid-range diversity ⇒ higher AI.
  - **Punctuation variety** — humans use a wider, messier range (dashes, ellipses,
    semicolons, parentheses); AI leans on a narrow set. Low variety ⇒ higher AI.
- **Output:** JSON `{ "ai_likelihood": 0.0–1.0, "features": {…raw metrics…} }`.
- **Blind spot:** short text (< ~3 sentences) has too little signal; deliberately
  uniform human writing (technical docs, listicles, simple poems) can score AI-ish.

### Combining the signals
`ai_likelihood = 0.6 * llm_score + 0.4 * stylometric_score`

The LLM gets the higher weight because it is the more informative signal on typical
prose; stylometry is the independent structural check that keeps the LLM honest and
degrades gracefully when the API is unavailable (its weight is renormalized to 1.0
if the LLM call fails).

## 3. Uncertainty Representation (Spec Question 2)

- `ai_likelihood ∈ [0,1]` is the system's estimate that the text is AI-generated.
- **`confidence`** reported to the user = `certainty`, defined as how far the score
  sits from the maximally-uncertain midpoint:
  `certainty = 2 * abs(ai_likelihood − 0.5)` (0 at 0.5, 1.0 at the extremes).
  A score of **0.6** therefore means "leans AI, but only `certainty = 0.2` — barely
  above a coin flip," which is exactly the honest thing to tell a user.
- **Thresholds (attribution buckets):**
  - `ai_likelihood ≥ 0.65` → **likely_ai**
  - `0.35 < ai_likelihood < 0.65` → **uncertain**
  - `ai_likelihood ≤ 0.35` → **likely_human**
- These are deliberately *not* a binary flip at 0.5. The wide `uncertain` band
  (0.35–0.65) is where borderline scores like 0.6 land, so a marginal result never
  masquerades as a firm accusation.

---

## 4. Transparency Label Design (Spec Question 3)

Three variants, each keyed to the attribution bucket. `{pct}` is
`round(ai_likelihood * 100)`.

- **High-confidence AI** (`likely_ai`):
  > "⚑ Likely AI-generated. Our analysis estimates a {pct}% chance this text was
  > produced by AI, based on two independent signals. If you believe this is wrong,
  > you can appeal using your content ID."

- **High-confidence human** (`likely_human`):
  > "✓ Likely human-written. Our analysis found no strong signs of AI generation
  > ({pct}% AI-likelihood). This is an estimate, not a guarantee."

- **Uncertain** (`uncertain`):
  > "? Inconclusive. Our signals disagree or are weak ({pct}% AI-likelihood, low
  > certainty). We are not confident either way; treat this result with caution and
  > appeal if it affects you."

---

## 5. Appeals Workflow (Spec Question 4)

- **Who:** the creator of a submission, identified by the `content_id` they received.
- **What they provide:** `POST /appeal` with `{ content_id, creator_id, reason }`.
- **What the system does:**
  1. Looks up the record by `content_id` (404 if unknown).
  2. Sets that record's `status` from `classified` → `appealed`, stores the reason.
  3. Writes a new audit-log entry (`event: "appeal"`) so the original classification
     row is preserved and the appeal is an additional, timestamped fact.
  4. Returns the updated status to the creator.
- **Reviewer view (`GET /appeals`):** the queue of records with `status = appealed`,
  each showing content id, creator id, original attribution + scores, the appeal
  reason, and timestamps — everything a human needs to overturn or uphold the call.

---

## 6. Anticipated Edge Cases (Spec Question 5)

1. **Short poem with repetition and simple vocabulary** ("Roses are red…"). Low
   sentence-length variance + low type-token ratio push the *stylometric* signal
   toward AI, even though a human wrote it. Mitigation: the LLM signal usually
   disagrees, and short text (< 3 sentences) triggers a low-confidence penalty so it
   lands in `uncertain` rather than `likely_ai`.
2. **Human-edited AI draft.** A person lightly rewrites ChatGPT output. The LLM may
   still smell the AI register while stylometry now looks human — the signals
   disagree, correctly producing an `uncertain` label instead of a false certainty.
3. **Very long, formal technical documentation written by a human.** Uniform
   sentence structure and controlled vocabulary make it *look* statistically like AI;
   this is a known false-positive risk the wide uncertain band is designed to catch.

## Architecture

### Submission flow
```
POST /submit {text, creator_id}
        │  raw text
        ▼
  [Request layer]  ── validate + rate-limit ──►
        │  raw text
        ├───────────────► [Signal 1: Groq LLM]  ── llm_score (0–1) + rationale ─┐
        │  raw text                                                             │
        └───────────────► [Signal 2: Stylometry] ── stylo_score (0–1) + feats ──┤
                                                                                ▼
                                                       [Scoring layer]  combined ai_likelihood
                                                                        + certainty + attribution
                                                                                │
                                                                                ▼
                                                       [Label layer]  transparency label text
                                                                                │
                          ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
                          ▼                                                       ▼
                   [Audit log: SQLite]  <── structured row ──          Response {content_id,
                   content_id, scores, attribution,                    attribution, confidence,
                   label, status=classified, ts                        label, signals}
```

### Appeal flow
```
POST /appeal {content_id, creator_id, reason}
        │  content_id + reason
        ▼
  [Appeal handler]  ── look up record ──► status: classified → appealed
        │                                       │
        │  appeal event                         │  updated status
        ▼                                       ▼
  [Audit log: SQLite]  <── new "appeal" row ──   Response {content_id, status: appealed}
        │
        ▼
  GET /appeals  ── reviewer queue: all status=appealed with scores + reason
```

**Narrative:** On submission, raw text fans out to both signals in parallel; their
scores are combined into one calibrated `ai_likelihood`, turned into an attribution
bucket and a transparency label, logged, and returned with the `content_id`. On
appeal, the creator sends that `content_id`; the handler flips the record's status to
`appealed`, appends an audit entry (the original classification is never overwritten),
and the record appears in the reviewer queue.

  diagram + §5-ish API contract.

  the `groq_signal(text)` function returning `{ai_likelihood, rationale}`, and the
  SQLite audit-log helper + `GET /log`.
- *Verify:* call `groq_signal()` directly on 2–3 clearly-human and clearly-AI samples
  and inspect the raw JSON before wiring it into the route; then curl `/submit` and
  confirm `content_id`, `attribution`, and a structured `/log` entry.

**Milestone 4 — Signal 2 + confidence scoring**
- *Spec provided:* §2 (Signal 2) + §3 Uncertainty Representation + the diagram.
- *Ask for:* the pure-Python `stylometric_signal(text)` function and the
  `combine_signals()` / attribution-bucketing logic from §3.
- *Check:* clearly-AI vs clearly-human text produces meaningfully different
  `ai_likelihood` and lands in the right bucket; a borderline sample lands in
  `uncertain`, not a hard flip at 0.5.

- *Spec provided:* §4 Label Design + §5 Appeals Workflow + the appeal diagram.

  returned `content_id` and confirm status flips to `appealed` and a new audit row
  appears.
